A Short History

01 — TIMELINE
1964

Blue Ribbon Sports

University of Oregon runner Phil Knight and his coach Bill Bowerman put up $500 each to start importing Japanese running shoes from Onitsuka (later ASICS) under the name Blue Ribbon Sports. For years the company operated out of the trunk of Knight's car at track meets.

1971

The Nike name and the Swoosh

As the partnership with Onitsuka broke down, the company needed its own brand. It took the name Nike, after the Greek goddess of victory, and paid design student Carolyn Davidson $35 for a checkmark-like logo that became the Swoosh.

1972

The waffle sole

Bowerman famously poured rubber into his wife's waffle iron to experiment with a lightweight, high-traction outsole. The resulting waffle pattern debuted on the Nike Cortez and Waffle Trainer and became a defining feature of early Nike running shoes.

1978

Nike Air arrives

Engineer Frank Rudy convinced Nike to encase pressurized gas in a polyurethane sole unit. The Tailwind, released in 1978, was the first shoe to use this "Nike Air" cushioning — the technology that would define the brand for the next two decades.

1982

Air Force 1

Designed by Bruce Kilgore, the Air Force 1 was the first basketball shoe to feature Nike Air cushioning. It fell out of production within a few years but returned in 1986 after retailer demand, becoming one of the best-selling sneakers of all time.

1985

Air Jordan 1

Nike signed a young Chicago Bulls rookie named Michael Jordan and built a signature shoe around him, designed by Peter Moore, Bruce Kilgore, and Tinker Hatfield's team. The NBA reportedly fined Jordan for wearing the black-and-red colorway, which only fueled its popularity.

1987

Air Max 1

Designer Tinker Hatfield, inspired by the exposed steel structure of Paris's Pompidou Centre, cut a window into the midsole so the Air unit was visible for the first time. It launched alongside the "Revolution" ad campaign set to the Beatles song of the same name.

1988

"Just Do It"

Nike's advertising agency Wieden+Kennedy launched the "Just Do It" campaign, which grew the company from a running-shoe specialist into a global lifestyle and culture brand over the following decade.

1991

Air Huarache

Inspired by neoprene water-ski booties, the Huarache introduced a snug internal sleeve wrapped by an external strap-and-cage structure, stripping the sneaker down to what Tinker Hatfield called its essential support elements.

2012

Flyknit

Nike introduced Flyknit, a knitting process that produced a one-piece, sock-like upper with almost no cut fabric waste. It debuted on a racing shoe ahead of the London Olympics and has since spread across running, training, and lifestyle lines.

2017

Vaporfly and the marathon shoe boom

The Zoom Vaporfly 4% combined a curved carbon-fiber plate with a new lightweight foam, sparking a wave of "super shoes" across distance running and reshaping marathon world-record times over the following seasons.

Today

Retro cycles and material research

Much of Nike's current output is a mix of retro reissues of models like the Dunk, Air Max, and Air Jordan line, signature shoes for current athletes, and ongoing material research through initiatives like Move to Zero. The section below tracks what's actually shipping this week.

Ten Defining Models

02 — SILHOUETTES
01

Cortez

1972

The waffle-soled runner that helped launch the brand and later crossed into streetwear via film and West Coast culture.

02

Blazer

1972

One of Nike's first basketball shoes and one of the earliest to carry the Swoosh, still a skate and streetwear staple.

03

Air Force 1

1982

The first Air-cushioned basketball shoe. Its plain, high-top silhouette became a blank canvas for endless colorways.

04

Air Jordan 1

1985

Michael Jordan's debut signature shoe, and the model that turned athlete sneakers into a cultural and resale phenomenon.

05

Dunk

1985

Originally a college-team basketball shoe, later adopted by skaters in the "SB" line and now one of Nike's most reissued retros.

06

Air Max 1

1987

The first shoe with a visible Air unit, kicking off a running sub-brand that's still expanding nearly four decades later.

07

Air Huarache

1991

A neoprene-sleeve construction that influenced Nike's approach to fit and minimalism for years afterward.

08

Air Foamposite One

1997

A molded, seamless upper that looked like nothing else on shelves and became a favorite among collectors for its bold shapes.

09

Free Run

2004

Grew out of Nike's research into barefoot running biomechanics, built around a flexible, siped outsole.

10

Flyknit Racer

2012

Introduced Nike's knitting process to the world and previewed the lightweight, seamless uppers now common across the lineup.
